Overview

Architectural Adaptability and Environmental Performance

This master's thesis investigated strategies for architectural adaptability and environmental performance across diverse climatic and geographic contexts. The aim was to develop an adaptable Attefall house concept that minimises environmental impact through design flexibility and material optimisation.

My Focus

My primary focus was on adaptability, user experience, and architectural design. I analysed and compared climatic data from three test sites in Norway and Sweden, formulating location-specific design strategies.

To evaluate adaptability, I applied the Human-Centred Facade Simulation developed by Chris Mackey using Grasshopper and Ladybug Tools, assessing daylight performance, view quality, solar gains, and natural ventilation across varying orientations.

Design Concept

Architectural references were drawn from prefabricated wooden cabin typologies, emphasising low embodied emissions, modular construction, and material efficiency. The resulting design proposed a compact, prefabricated cabin system featuring interchangeable façade elements, enabling occupants to optimise energy use, views, and comfort within small-scale living environments.

LCA Integration

A Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) — conducted by Ida Hallebrand — was integrated throughout the design process to evaluate embodied emissions, material replacement cycles, and operational energy demand. Simulation tools further informed the building's energy and environmental performance, supporting a holistic approach that bridges adaptive architecture and sustainable design practice.
